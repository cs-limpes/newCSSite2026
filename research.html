Research & Writing

Trauma, somatics, chronic illness, altered states, and human systems.

Current Direction

My current academic direction is clinical counseling and psychology, with a focus on trauma, somatic approaches, chronic illness, embodied experience, and psychedelic studies.

I am especially interested in how people live inside systems that shape their bodies, options, sense of self, and ability to participate. That includes nervous systems and families, but also institutions, technologies, cultural narratives, economic structures, medical systems, and spiritual or meaning-making frameworks.

My writing often sits between practical experience and research inquiry. I am drawn to questions that are personal, systemic, embodied, and difficult to separate cleanly into one discipline.

Working Questions

How do people make meaning from experiences that disrupt the body, identity, memory, belonging, or agency?

What can somatic and body-based approaches offer people navigating trauma, chronic illness, pain, dissociation, grief, or nervous system dysregulation?

How do institutions and technologies shape what kinds of healing, participation, and knowledge are treated as legitimate?

What happens when lived experience is treated not as anecdotal noise, but as a form of situated knowledge?

Through-line

The work keeps returning to lived systems.

Whether I am writing about trauma, chronic illness, technology, access, labor, or healing, the central question is often the same: what kind of system is this, what does it ask of people, and what becomes possible or impossible inside it?
