Research & Writing

Trauma, somatics, chronic illness, altered states, and human systems.

Current Direction

My current academic direction is in clinical counseling and psychology, with a focus on trauma, somatic approaches, chronic illness, embodied experience, and psychedelic studies. Alongside that formal path, I maintain independent research threads on AI, labor, hiring systems, productivity narratives, meaning-making, time, and the ways people live inside systems that shape their bodies, choices, and sense of reality.

I am especially interested in how people live inside systems that shape their bodies, options, sense of self, and ability to participate. That includes nervous systems and families, but also institutions, technologies, cultural narratives, economic structures, medical systems, and spiritual or meaning-making frameworks.

My writing often sits between practical experience and research inquiry. I am drawn to questions that are personal, systemic, embodied, and difficult to separate cleanly into one discipline.

Working Questions

How do systems shape what people can access, understand, trust, feel, and become?

What happens when technologies promise ease, efficiency, or liberation, but instead redistribute hidden labor, uncertainty, or risk?

How do people make meaning from experiences that disrupt the body, identity, memory, belonging, agency, or sense of time?

How do feelings, observations, culture, and lived experience interact in the way people decide what is real?

What kinds of healing, participation, and knowledge are treated as legitimate, and who is excluded when institutions define those terms too narrowly?

What changes when time is understood as cyclical, relational, embodied, ecological, or seasonal rather than strictly linear and productivity-driven?

Independent Research Threads

Labor, technology, hiring systems

The Current Job Market and AI-Mediated Hiring

An ongoing inquiry into how AI is reshaping the job market, hiring practices, employer expectations, job seeker behavior, ghost jobs, applicant screening, labor narratives, investment pressure, and the experience of trying to become legible inside automated systems.

Psychology, perception, culture

Feeling, Reality, and Meaning-Making

A developing question about whether some people tend to treat feelings as evidence about reality while others observe reality first and allow those observations to shape emotional response. This thread considers culture, cognition, relational patterns, conflict, belief formation, and the consequences of different meaning-making styles.

AI systems, media tracking, public discourse

AI News, Development, and Social Adoption

An ongoing tracking project focused on AI development, platform shifts, public narratives, social impacts, workplace adoption, and the gap between technical capability, public marketing, and lived consequences. This thread also informs News of the AI and related public writing.

AI, labor, productivity, institutional promises

AI and the Productivity Promise

A focused inquiry into whether organizations are seeing the productivity gains promised by AI adoption, how those gains are measured, who benefits from them, and whether increased automation actually reduces labor or simply redistributes hidden work. This thread connects to Everything Was Supposed to Get Easier and Marketing Ambitions and Empirical Realities.

Somatics, chronic illness, autoimmune experience

Somatics, Chronic Pain, and Chronic Illness

An interdisciplinary inquiry into somatic practice, chronic pain, chronic illness, autoimmune disease, trauma, nervous system regulation, embodiment, dissociation, fatigue, and the ways people make meaning inside bodies that do not behave according to standard expectations.

Time, culture, embodiment, daily life

Alternative Conceptions of Time

A research thread exploring non-linear, cyclical, seasonal, relational, ritual, ecological, and embodied conceptions of time, with a particular interest in whether alternative theories of time actually change how people live, work, heal, plan, remember, and relate.

Through-line

The work keeps returning to lived systems.

Whether I am writing about trauma, chronic illness, AI, labor, time, access, productivity, or healing, the central question is often the same: what kind of system is this, what does it ask of people, and what becomes possible or impossible inside it?
